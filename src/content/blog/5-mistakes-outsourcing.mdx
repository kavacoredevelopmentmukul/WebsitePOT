# 5 Mistakes Companies Make When Outsourcing Development

Over the last decade, I've spoken with hundreds of CTOs and engineering leaders. A common theme emerges: many have a "horror story" about outsourcing. Code that had to be completely rewritten, missed deadlines, and complete communication breakdowns.

However, the problem usually isn't the concept of global talent; it's the execution. When outsourcing fails, it almost always traces back to one of these five critical mistakes.

## Mistake 1: Treating Them Like an "Agency" Instead of a "Team"

The biggest paradigm shift a company needs to make is moving from a project-based mindset to a team-based mindset. If you toss a PDF of requirements over the fence to a cheap offshore agency and say "build this," you will be disappointed. 

**The Fix:** Use a dedicated staff augmentation model. The developers should attend your daily standups, use your Slack, and feel like actual employees of your company. They aren't a vendor building a project; they are your team members writing code.

## Mistake 2: Ignoring Timezone Overlap

Trying to collaborate with a 12-hour timezone difference where you have zero overlapping working hours is a recipe for blocked workflows. An engineer gets stuck on a problem at 2 AM your time, sends a question, and has to wait until the next day for an answer. This kills velocity.

**The Fix:** Ensure your global talent partner offers flexible working hours. At Pacific Ocean Tech, our engineers shift their schedules to ensure a minimum of 4-5 hours of overlap with US or European business hours.

## Mistake 3: Under-investing in Onboarding

Because offshore developers are often viewed as "plug and play," companies fail to properly onboard them. They don't explain the business context, the user personas, or the architectural history of the codebase.

**The Fix:** Onboard remote global talent exactly as you would a local hire in your office. Walk them through the product vision, introduce them to the wider team, and assign them an internal "buddy" for their first two weeks. Context is crucial for writing good code.

## Mistake 4: Filtering Only by Cost

If you go to a freelance marketplace and filter by the lowest hourly rate, you are going to get exactly what you pay for: junior developers, poor communication skills, and spaghetti code. The goal of building a remote team shouldn't be to find the absolute cheapest labor on earth; it should be to find world-class talent at a *better* rate than your local market.

**The Fix:** Focus on value, not just cost. Partner with a firm that aggressively vets their talent. A senior engineer in India will cost more than a junior freelancer, but they will still be 60% cheaper than a US hire—and they will actually deliver enterprise-grade code.

## Mistake 5: Failing to Integrate QA and DevOps

Companies often hire offshore developers to write features, but leave QA and deployment entirely to their local team. This creates a massive bottleneck. The local team becomes overwhelmed with reviewing and testing code, slowing down the entire release cycle.

**The Fix:** Hire cross-functional pods. If you are hiring three backend developers, hire one QA automation engineer to support them. The remote team should be capable of taking a feature from Jira ticket all the way to a tested, deployed state.

By avoiding these five mistakes, global talent stops being a risk and becomes your company's greatest competitive advantage.
