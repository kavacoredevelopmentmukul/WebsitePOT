# 5 Mistakes Companies Make When Outsourcing Development

Over the last decade, I've spoken with hundreds of CTOs and engineering leaders. A common theme emerges: many have a "horror story" about outsourcing. Code that had to be completely rewritten, missed deadlines, and complete communication breakdowns.

However, the problem usually isn't the concept of global talent; it's the execution. When outsourcing fails, it almost always traces back to one of these five critical mistakes. Understanding them won't just save you money — it will turn global talent into your company's single greatest competitive advantage.

## Mistake 1: Treating Them Like an "Agency" Instead of a "Team"

The biggest paradigm shift a company needs to make is moving from a project-based mindset to a team-based mindset. If you toss a PDF of requirements over the fence to a cheap offshore agency and say "build this," you will be disappointed.

The project-based model incentivises the wrong behaviours. The agency wants to minimise hours spent (to protect margins), while you want maximum quality and iteration. These goals are fundamentally opposed. The result is rushed code, minimal documentation, and engineers who have no investment in your product's long-term success because they'll be reassigned to another client next month.

### The Fix

Use a dedicated [staff augmentation](/WebsitePOT/services/software-development) model. The developers should attend your daily standups, use your Slack, and feel like actual employees of your company. They aren't a vendor building a project; they are your team members writing code. When engineers know they'll be working on your product for the long haul, they make architectural decisions that prioritise maintainability over shortcuts — and that difference compounds over months and years.

## Mistake 2: Ignoring Timezone Overlap

Trying to collaborate with a 12-hour timezone difference where you have zero overlapping working hours is a recipe for blocked workflows. An engineer gets stuck on a problem at 2 AM your time, sends a question, and has to wait until the next day for an answer. This kills velocity.

The impact goes beyond individual blockers. Without real-time overlap, code reviews pile up, design discussions happen asynchronously over days instead of minutes, and sprint ceremonies become performative rather than productive. Teams that lack overlap tend to develop a "throw it over the wall" dynamic where handoffs replace collaboration.

### The Fix

Ensure your global talent partner offers flexible working hours. At Pacific Ocean Tech, our engineers shift their schedules to ensure a minimum of 4–5 hours of overlap with US, European, or Australian business hours. This overlap window is sacred — it's when pair programming, live code reviews, and the informal conversations that build team cohesion actually happen. The remaining hours are for deep, uninterrupted focus work.

## Mistake 3: Under-investing in Onboarding

Because offshore developers are often viewed as "plug and play," companies fail to properly onboard them. They don't explain the business context, the user personas, or the architectural history of the codebase. The new engineer is given a Jira ticket on day one and expected to start producing immediately.

This is a false economy. An engineer who doesn't understand *why* the code exists the way it does will inevitably make decisions that conflict with the product's direction. They'll refactor code that was written intentionally, introduce patterns that clash with existing conventions, and ask questions that slow down the rest of the team because nobody invested the time to give them context upfront.

### The Fix

Onboard remote global talent exactly as you would a local hire in your office. Walk them through the product vision, introduce them to the wider team, and assign them an internal "buddy" for their first two weeks. Share your architecture decision records, your deployment runbooks, and your incident post-mortems. Context is crucial for writing good code, and investing 5 days in onboarding saves 50 days of rework later. ## Mistake 4: Filtering Only by Cost

If you go to a freelance marketplace and filter by the lowest hourly rate, you are going to get exactly what you pay for: junior developers, poor communication skills, and spaghetti code. The goal of building a remote team shouldn't be to find the absolute cheapest labour on earth; it should be to find world-class talent at a *better* rate than your local market.

The maths is counterintuitive but important: a senior engineer at $60/hour who delivers production-ready code in 40 hours is dramatically cheaper than a junior developer at $20/hour who takes 200 hours and produces code that needs to be rewritten. Quality engineers write fewer bugs, require less oversight, and make better architectural decisions — all of which compound into real cost savings over time.

### The Fix

Focus on value, not just cost. Partner with a firm that aggressively vets their talent. A senior engineer sourced from the Pacific Region will cost more than a junior freelancer, but they will still be 60% cheaper than a US hire — and they will actually deliver enterprise-grade code. Look for partners who publish their [vetting process](/WebsitePOT/about) and can demonstrate measurable quality standards, not just headcount.

## Mistake 5: Failing to Integrate QA and DevOps

Companies often hire offshore developers to write features, but leave QA and deployment entirely to their local team. This creates a massive bottleneck. The local team becomes overwhelmed with reviewing and testing code, slowing down the entire release cycle.

The deeper problem is accountability. When the remote team's responsibility ends at "code written," they have no incentive to ensure that code actually works in production. They never see the bugs their code causes, never feel the pain of a failed deployment, and never develop the production-awareness that separates good engineers from great ones.

### The Fix

Hire cross-functional pods. If you are hiring three backend developers, hire one QA automation engineer to support them. The remote team should be capable of taking a feature from Jira ticket all the way to a tested, deployed state. This end-to-end ownership means the team that writes the code is the team that ships the code — and the team that gets paged when it breaks. That accountability loop is the single most effective quality improvement you can make.

## The Bottom Line

By avoiding these five mistakes, global talent stops being a risk and becomes your company's greatest competitive advantage. The companies that get outsourcing right don't think of it as outsourcing at all — they think of it as building a distributed engineering organisation that happens to span multiple time zones.
