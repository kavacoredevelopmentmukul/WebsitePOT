# How to Build an AI Engineering Team in 30 Days

The race for AI dominance has created an unprecedented bottleneck: talent. According to recent industry reports, the average time to hire a senior machine learning engineer in San Francisco or New York is now pushing 5 months. For startups and enterprises trying to deploy generative AI or complex RAG pipelines, a 5-month delay can mean losing market leadership entirely.

Building an AI team from scratch doesn't have to take half a year. By rethinking *where* and *how* you source talent, you can assemble a world-class AI engineering pod in just 30 days. Here is the blueprint we've used to help dozens of companies go from zero to production-ready AI teams.

## Day 1–5: Define the Architecture, Not Just the Roles

The biggest mistake companies make is writing generic job descriptions ("Need a Data Scientist"). AI is highly specialised. Before looking for people, you must define the architecture.

### Start With the Problem, Not the Technology

Ask yourself three questions before writing a single job spec:

- Are you fine-tuning open-source LLMs like Llama 3 or Mistral?
- Are you building a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system with a vector database like Pinecone or Weaviate?
- Do you need MLOps infrastructure to deploy and monitor models at scale?

By defining the architecture first, you can pinpoint the exact skill sets required. A standard AI pod for a new product might look like:

1. **Lead AI Architect** — System design, model selection, eval framework design
2. **Data Engineer** — Pipeline creation, data cleaning, embedding generation
3. **MLOps Engineer** — Deployment infrastructure, monitoring, cost optimisation
4. **Backend Developer** — API integration, serving layer, authentication

This specificity matters because a generalist "ML engineer" job posting attracts hundreds of misaligned applicants. When you know you need someone who has built production RAG with citation grounding and confidence scoring, you can evaluate candidates against that precise requirement — and your interview conversion rate skyrockets.

## Day 16–23: Accelerated Interview Sprints

When the candidates are pre-vetted, your interview process changes from "Can they code?" to "Can they solve *our* specific problem?"

### A Two-Round Interview That Actually Works

Limit your interview process to two rounds:

1. **System Design & Architecture (1 hour):** Have the candidate walk through how they would build your specific AI feature. Give them your actual constraints — latency requirements, data volume, cost targets — and evaluate their trade-off reasoning, not just their technical knowledge. A great AI engineer will push back on unnecessary complexity and propose the simplest architecture that meets the requirements.

2. **Cultural Fit & Communication (45 minutes):** Assess how they communicate complex technical concepts to non-technical stakeholders. This is especially important for AI engineers, who need to explain model limitations, confidence scores, and failure modes to product managers and executives who may not have ML backgrounds.

Because you are interviewing from a pre-vetted pool, the conversion rate from interview to offer is dramatically higher than traditional hiring. We typically see clients extend offers to 60–70% of the candidates we present, compared to the industry average of 15–20%.
